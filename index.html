Executive Summary

The Bridge Program is a structured, cohort-based peer learning exchange between Phiture and Precis. Running from June to September 2026, it pairs 60 intentionally selected employees — 30 from Phiture, 30 from Precis — in 1:1 cross-company pairings designed to drive real skill transfer, not networking in disguise.

⇄

Bilateral Skill Exchange

Every participant both teaches and learns. No hierarchy. No mentors. Sessions alternate direction.

◎

Meaningful Relationships

60-minute structured sessions over 12 weeks build depth that a networking event never can.

✦

Cultural Integration

Grounded in professional credibility, not forced socialisation. Identity is preserved, not erased.

Key milestone The program culminates at the Precis Festival in September 2026, where all 30 pairs meet in person for the first time — converting months of structured digital collaboration into a real, lasting connection.

The program is designed to run with minimal ongoing HR involvement after kickoff. It requires one dedicated coordinator (0.2 FTE), simple digital infrastructure, and leadership sponsorship from both companies.

Program Concept

What it is

The Bridge Program is a 12-week structured peer learning exchange. Participants are paired across company boundaries and commit to a defined cadence of skill-sharing sessions. Each pair produces a reciprocal exchange: Person A teaches Person B something; Person B teaches Person A something. Neither role is senior or junior — both sides contribute equally.

What this is not This is not mentoring — there is no mentor and mentee. It is not networking — there are no coffees disguised as development. Every session has a learning objective set in advance and a short reflection logged afterwards.

Why it is differentiated

Most cross-company integration efforts fail because they are either too social ("get to know each other" events) or too operational ("align on process" workshops). The Bridge Program is neither. It creates a structured reason to spend recurring, substantive time with someone from the other company — and that reason is learning something specific that you cannot learn inside your own team.

The post-acquisition context makes this urgent. Phiture employees need to understand how Precis operates at scale. Precis employees need to understand Phiture's craft expertise in mobile growth. Both sides have knowledge the other lacks. The program creates the channel for that exchange to happen deliberately.

Design principles

  • Every interaction must have a learning outcome. If a session cannot be described in terms of what was taught and learned, it does not count as a session.
  • Reciprocal by design. No hierarchy between participants — both sides teach, both sides learn.
  • Small enough to be intentional. 60 participants max in Cohort 1. Quality over scale.
  • Anchored to a real in-person moment. The September Festival gives the programme a climax and a reason to sustain engagement.
  • Self-sustaining after kickoff. Pairs own their own schedule and agenda — HR is not the engine.

Why Model A is recommended for Cohort 1

  • Deeper relationships. 1:1 conversations go further than group dynamics allow. You cannot be a passive listener in a pair.
  • Simpler to match. One dimension less than triads — bilateral skill alignment is much easier to optimise with 30 pairs than three-way alignment with 20 triads.
  • Clearer accountability. When something goes wrong (a pair drifts, sessions stop), there are two people responsible — easy to intervene.
  • Easier to measure. Impact is attributable to a specific pairing, not a group dynamic.
  • Right for a first cohort. Prove the model before scaling.
Decision Launch Cohort 1 as 1:1 pairs. Introduce triads in Cohort 2 (2027) as a complementary track for participants who have already completed a 1:1 cycle and want a group learning format.

Participant Selection

Cohort size and split

Cohort 1 targets 60 participants: 30 from Phiture (27% of the company) and 30 from Precis (~6%). This is intentionally asymmetric — Phiture's full representation matters for cultural integration, while Precis participation must be filtered for relevance and motivation.

Eligibility — both companies

  • Minimum 6 months in current role — settled enough to have something to teach
  • No planned parental leave or major leave during June–September
  • Manager approval required — not to gatekeep, but to ensure workload supports the commitment
  • Willing to commit a minimum of 3 hours per month (sessions + prep + reflection)

Eligibility — Precis-specific filter

Not all of Precis can or should participate. The filtering criteria are stricter because Precis employees need a genuine reason to engage with Phiture — not just curiosity.

  • Works in a function with strategic overlap with Phiture: Performance, Data, Strategy, Creative, Product, or HR
  • Has expressed interest in mobile app marketing, ASO, or growth marketing in the past 12 months — OR has a cross-sell/upsell relationship with Phiture clients
  • Manager level or above, OR a specialist with 3+ years in their domain
  • Must be nominated by department head or self-select with department head endorsement

Matching Logic

Input data — Matching Profile

After selection, each participant completes a short Matching Profile (5 fields, in a form):

  • Primary skill to teach — one sentence, specific (e.g. "App Store Optimisation keyword strategy", not "marketing")
  • Primary skill to learn — one sentence, specific
  • Secondary skill to teach — optional but encouraged
  • Domain / function — team and area of work
  • Preferred working style — async-heavy / sync-heavy; structured / exploratory

Matching criteria and weights

Skill alignment — A teaches B40%
Skill alignment — B teaches A (bidirectional quality)30%
Cross-company & functional diversity30%

Each criterion is scored 0–5. The weighted total produces a match score out of 5.0. The coordinator reviews the top 3 candidates per person and makes the final call manually.

Manual override criteria

  • Seniority balance. Avoid very senior Precis + very junior Phiture (or vice versa) — it recreates a mentoring dynamic.
  • Client overlap. If A and B already interact on the same client account, choose a different pair.
  • Working style fit. If one is async-heavy and one is sync-heavy, flag but do not automatically reject — note it in the welcome message.

Score interpretation

ScoreInterpretationAction
4.0 – 5.0Excellent matchConfirm pair
3.0 – 3.9Good match — one criterion weakerConfirm; note the weaker criterion in welcome pack
2.0 – 2.9MarginalConfirm only if no better option; flag for Week 4 check-in
Below 2.0Poor matchDo not pair — return to pool or use waitlist candidate
Quality threshold 90% of pairs should score ≥3.0 out of 5.0. No pair should be made if the primary skill match score (criterion 1 alone) is below 2.0 — the core exchange has to work.

Pre-Festival Activation

The week before the Festival (1–7 September), each pair completes one joint activation task. This creates a concrete shared artefact the pair can reference when they meet in person — and ideally share with their teams afterwards.

The joint task: a 1-page knowledge transfer brief

Each pair co-authors a single document (1 page, any format) structured around three prompts:

  1. What I learned from my partner — 3 bullet points maximum, specific and applied. Not "I learned about ASO" but "I learned how to use search tab impression share to identify ranking ceilings."
  2. How I have or plan to use it — one concrete example from their own work or client context.
  3. One question I still have — something they hope the in-person conversation will answer or take further.
Why this matters Pairs who complete the pre-Festival brief arrive at the Festival with something specific to talk about — not "so, how has it been going?" but "I've been thinking about that question I still had, and I wanted to ask you about X." It converts awkwardness into momentum.

The coordinator sends a lightweight template the first week of September. Pairs have 5 working days to complete it. The document is not for publication — it is the pair's personal artefact and conversation starter.

In-Person Festival Integration

The Festival is the first time pairs meet in person. This moment needs to feel earned. The Bridge Program should have a distinct, identifiable presence within the Festival agenda — without consuming too much space or feeling like a separate track imposed on the event.

Touchpoint 1 — The Bridge Table (Day 1, Evening Social)

During the opening social, all 60 Bridge participants are seated at "Bridge Tables" — tables of 8–10 where at least 4 pairs are represented. No structured agenda. The coordinator places a simple card on each table with one prompt: "Tell your table what your partner taught you — in one sentence." This prompts organic conversation without forcing a format.

Touchpoint 2 — The Bridge Showcase (Day 2, 30-min slot)

3–4 pairs are invited (voluntarily) to give a 5-minute joint showcase of what they learned from each other. Format: one slide or whiteboard, no formal presentation required. Pairs are selected by the coordinator based on the quality of their Session 5 summaries. The showcase is visible to the full Festival audience — it signals what the programme achieved to people who were not part of it.

Touchpoint 3 — The Next Step card (Day 2 or 3)

Each pair receives a prompt card asking them to agree one thing they will do together after the Festival. Examples: share a relevant internal document, introduce each other to one colleague, do one optional additional session. The coordinator collects these commitments digitally on Day 3. This creates a lightweight accountability moment without overcommitting anyone.

What to avoid

  • Do not run a formal Bridge Program breakout session that excludes non-participants — it signals exclusivity in a negative way
  • Do not require participants to present or share publicly — the showcase must be entirely voluntary
  • Do not use the Festival moment to evaluate the programme — it is a celebration, not a review

Post-Program Continuation

Alumni track — October to December

  • All 60 participants join a dedicated Slack channel: #bridge-alumni
  • Coordinator posts one prompt per month — a question or challenge to spark cross-company knowledge sharing (e.g. "What is one thing your pair taught you that you have now taught someone else on your team?")
  • One optional "Alumni Hour" virtual session in November — open format, no agenda except reconnection and peer sharing
  • Pairs who want to continue meeting 1:1 are encouraged but not tracked — at this point it becomes self-sustaining

Cohort 2 pipeline

In December, the coordinator launches a brief expression-of-interest form for Cohort 2 (targeting Q1 2027). Alumni from Cohort 1 are invited to become peer ambassadors — they help promote the programme internally and can optionally be matched with a new participant as a companion (not a re-run of the full programme).

Institutional knowledge capture

The Session 5 joint summaries, pre-Festival briefs, and end-of-program survey data are compiled into a one-page Cohort 1 Impact Report. This is shared with leadership from both companies and is the primary input for deciding whether to run Cohort 2 and whether to scale to 90 participants.

What makes this programme succeed or fail

Success factors

What drives it

  • Intentional selection — every participant has a real reason to be there
  • Specific matching — pairs are made because the skills actually align
  • Structured sessions — a clear format prevents sessions from drifting to catch-up calls
  • Leadership visibility — sponsors name the programme publicly at All Hands
  • The Festival — gives pairs a deadline and a shared goal
Risk factors

What kills it

  • Open applications — quality collapses if anyone can join
  • Vague session design — without structure, pairs run out of things to say by Session 3
  • No coordinator follow-up — pairs who miss Session 1 rarely catch up
  • Framing it as networking — participants disengage when it feels like HR box-ticking
  • Scaling too fast — Cohort 1 must work before Cohort 2 is launched
